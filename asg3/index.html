Better UX Through Storytelling — How framing helps refine ideas

Repurposing the project framework to a story gives me an opportunity to clarify ideas. I have to name a user, a goal, the setting, a conflict that prevents the user from achieving the goal they want, and what my product provides as a resolution to the user's conflict. Revisiting each feature as scenes (setup → action → outcome) helps identify gaps in the product, edge cases that I had not considered, and aspects that I can remove because they are unnecessary. It also allows me to surface and clarify the few moments that matter. A story also helps draw out the stakes and emotions that help inform the hierarchy of content, tone, and pathways of interaction. Finally, the story helps provide testable hypotheses. Each scene is something I can prototype, test, iterate on, and measure; and it allows me to shift from a fuzzy feature list to a more circumscribed and user-focused journey. case (ALL CAPS vs. lowercase), position and whitespace, alignment, spacing (letter-spacing/tracking and line-height/leading), and consistent patterns (e.g., H1 → H2 → body) so users learn the rhythm.

Meet Your Type — Establishing Typographic Hierarchy

Ways to create a hierarchy/emphasis: size/scale, weight (bold/light), typeface or family/variant, color and contrast, case (ALL CAPS vs. lower case), position and white space, alignment, spacing (letter-spacing/tracking and line-heights/leading), and consistent patterns (e.g., H1 → H2 → body) so that users learn the rhythm.

StudyScape — Campus Quiet Zone Map

A web application that serves as a map for students, showing locations of quiet, pleasant study spaces on campus or close by. The app has filters for identifying a specific study space, such as "outdoor," "with outlets," "coffee nearby," or "fridge available." Students can also rate study spaces and upload images. It is accessible as a web app, so students don't need to download anything to browse and go.

Problem

Students waste time searching for suitable study spaces. Students will have information about noise-levels, outlets, seating, and nearby amenities, but it is typically sourced from one-off word of mouth (often limited) or aimed at a wider audience (for example Reddit threads that orient around question/answer format), or out of date blog posts or articles; leading to overprotection for study/learning spaces, wasted study time, and of course overcrowding.

Audience

  • Undergraduate and graduate students that rely on space between classes for quiet and more predictable study spaces while on campus.
  • New students or commuters that don't know most campus buildings or other spaces close by.
  • Neurodivergent learners that benefit from predictable sensory environments.
  • Study groups looking for spaces with tables, outlets and open longer hours.
